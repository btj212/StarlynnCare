## The two lead findings (live, citable, sourced)

### 1. Price ≠ Quality in California memory care
**URL:** https://starlynncare.com/research/california-cost-vs-quality  
**Published:** May 11, 2026

Across **484 California memory care facilities**, the correlation between county-median monthly cost and inspection-derived citation severity is **r = 0.27** — statistically detectable but too weak to use as a proxy for safety in either direction.

Key numbers (all from CDSS CCL + Genworth 2024 Cost of Care Survey):

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| Facilities analyzed | 484 California RCFEs |
| Price ↔ severity correlation (Pearson) | r = 0.27 (p < 0.001) |
| Facilities with severity index > 5 | 13% (63 of 484) |
| Most expensive county | San Francisco ($8,200/month median) |
| Highest average citation severity | Contra Costa County (index = 4.44) |

The finding that makes this pitchable: **the most expensive county and the worst-cited county are different counties.** A family paying $8,200/month in San Francisco is not buying safety relative to a family in a less-expensive market; the data does not support that assumption.

**Quotable finding:** "Paying more does not move you out of risk. 13% of California memory care facilities carry a serious citation pattern, and they are spread across the state's price spectrum, not concentrated at the bottom."

---

### 2. High-income ZIP codes don't have better inspection records
**URL:** https://starlynncare.com/research/california-geographic-equity  
**Published:** May 11, 2026

ZIP-level household income explains almost none of the variation in California memory care deficiency rates — **Pearson r = 0.23**.

Key numbers (CDSS CCL + ACS Census income data):

| Income quartile | Avg. deficiency rate per inspection |
|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| Q1 — Low income | 0.52 |
| Q2 — Mid-low | 0.71 |
| Q3 — Mid-high | 0.87 |
| Q4 — High income | **1.32** |

The highest-income ZIP quartile has the **highest average deficiency rate**. The effect is modest but the direction is the opposite of what families typically expect.

Specific high-income examples with serious citation rates:
- **Newark, CA (94560)** — median household income $159,465; deficiency rate 5.97
- **Fremont, CA (94555)** — median household income $202,832; deficiency rate 4.78

**Quotable finding:** "Several of the highest-income ZIP codes in the Bay Area rank among the worst-cited areas for memory care deficiencies. Wealthy neighborhoods do not automatically deliver safer facilities."

## The combined pitch

Together these two analyses make a single, actionable story for families: **the heuristics most people rely on — pay more, pick a nice neighborhood — are not validated by California's own inspection record.** The only way to know how a facility has performed is to read the record.

StarlynnCare makes that record readable in plain language for the first time: every citation, every complaint investigation, every enforcement action from the California Department of Social Services' Community Care Licensing division, at https://starlynncare.com/california/facilities.

## What we are / what we are not

- **Free, no commission, no paid placement.** Facilities do not pay to appear and cannot pay to rank higher. Full editorial policy: https://starlynncare.com/editorial-policy
- **Government data only.** Every inspection record links to the original CDSS source document.
- **YMYL-standard editorial practice.** No invented ratings, no aggregate star scores from thin data.
- **Not a lead-generation service.** We do not take referral fees or sell family contact information.
