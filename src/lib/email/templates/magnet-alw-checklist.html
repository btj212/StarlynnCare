Medi-Cal / ALW memory care checklist

County participation, waitlist steps, SSI room-and-board rates, and the facility questions to ask — in one printable reference.

What is the ALW program?

The Assisted Living Waiver (ALW) is a California Medi-Cal program that pays for residential care in an RCFE for people who would otherwise qualify for nursing home care. Spots are limited by county and facility — not all counties participate, and not all participating facilities have open slots. The program pays a daily rate directly to the facility; the resident's SSI covers the room-and-board portion.

Step 1 · Confirm county participation

☐ Look up your county on the DHCS ALW county list (dhcs.ca.gov) — not all counties participate
☐ Call the county's Medi-Cal managed care plan to ask if there are open ALW spots in your area
☐ Confirm the person is currently enrolled in or eligible for full-scope Medi-Cal (not just QMB or partial)

Step 2 · Level of care assessment

☐ Request a Medi-Cal level of care (LOC) assessment — the person must meet nursing facility level of care to qualify
☐ Get a copy of the assessment in writing — you'll need it when applying to facilities
☐ Ask: is the assessment current? (LOC assessments typically expire after 12 months)

Step 3 · Finding and qualifying a facility

☐ Ask every facility you contact: "Do you have an ALW contract with Medi-Cal?"
☐ Ask: "Do you currently have an open ALW bed?" (contracts don't guarantee availability)
☐ Confirm the room-and-board rate vs. the resident's SSI income — any shortfall is the resident's responsibility
☐ Ask: what does the ALW rate cover vs. what costs extra? (transportation, personal care supplies, activities)

Step 4 · Contract and admission agreement

☐ Request the admission agreement before signing anything — read it, don't skim it
☐ Flag any fee escalation clauses — these sometimes appear in ALW agreements despite the fixed Medi-Cal rate
☐ Look for the discharge policy — what happens if Medi-Cal eligibility lapses or the program is discontinued?
☐ Ask whether there is a mandatory arbitration clause — you can sometimes negotiate to remove it
