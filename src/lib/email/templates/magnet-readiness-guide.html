The readiness signs — and how to talk about them

For the family that isn't sure yet. This is not about pushing anyone — it's about knowing what to name when the moment comes.

Part 1 · The 8 safety signals

Any one of these is worth a serious conversation. Three or more happening at once is an escalation:

1. Unsafe wandering — getting lost in familiar places or leaving the home unsafely
2. Progressive weight loss from not completing meals, even with cueing
3. Medication errors — wrong doses, skipped doses, despite pill organizers
4. Caregiver physical injury — repeated lifting incidents or sleep deprivation
5. Hygiene failure the person can no longer compensate for even with reminders
6. Driving incidents — accidents, getting lost, or near-misses
7. A clinician recommending supervised care — in writing, not informally
8. Caregiver saying "I can't do this anymore" — take that seriously

Part 2 · The family conversation script

This is the hardest part — not the logistics, but talking to people who see things differently. A few openers that don't start a fight:

When a sibling thinks you're overreacting

"I'm not saying we decide today. I'm asking if we can agree that [specific incident] means we need to at least look at options. Can we do that?"

When your parent resists

"I know you want to stay home. I want to find a way to honor that as long as it's safe. Let's look at what safe actually requires right now."

When guilt is the dominant emotion in the room

"Moving to memory care isn't abandonment. It's choosing a level of care that we can't safely provide at home anymore. Those are different things."

Note: if there's any question about the person's capacity to make decisions, ask the primary care physician for a formal assessment. The conversation changes when clinicians have documented it.
